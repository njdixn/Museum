The feathered ceremonial headdress from Cave 2 was found alongside three other headdresses of differing materials and designs, each carefully placed on a low stone platform. While the recovered specimen features a hardwood frame, woven plant fiber bindings, macaw feathers, and shell inlay with spiral motifs, the others exhibited variations—one with predominantly black and white feathers arranged in a fan shape, another adorned with woven reed extensions and dyed fiber tassels, and a fourth crafted almost entirely from carved wooden panels painted in vivid pigments. The grouping suggests a deliberate arrangement, possibly representing a set of regalia used in distinct ceremonial roles.

The visual differences between them may have been a form of nonverbal communication within ritual practice. The macaw-feather headdress, with its vibrant red, yellow, and blue hues, could have signified vitality, seasonal abundance, or a leadership role tied to agricultural rites. In contrast, the black-and-white fan-shaped headdress may have been worn during funerary or transition rites, its stark contrast symbolizing life and death or the balance between worlds. The reed-and-tassel version might have been used in ceremonies related to water, fertility, or communal gatherings, while the painted wooden-panel headdress could have marked a storyteller, historian, or ritual officiant, its rigid structure implying authority and permanence.

The co-presence of these four objects in a single ceremonial context points toward a social or spiritual hierarchy in which each headdress was associated with a distinct role. They may have been worn by different members of a ritual order, each responsible for invoking specific deities, elements, or ancestral spirits. The stone platform where they were found could have served as a consecrated space for storing regalia between uses, imbuing the site with both practical and symbolic importance. Taken together, the set offers rare insight into a complex visual language where color, material, and form each contributed to layered meanings within the community’s ceremonial life.

The differences between the four headdresses could also symbolize complementary forces or realms—such as earth, sky, water, and the underworld—or represent seasonal cycles, with each piece reserved for rituals tied to planting, harvest, renewal, or appeasement of deities. In many ancient societies, this kind of symbolic diversity reinforced the idea that leadership and sacred duties were interconnected, and that harmony required the balance of multiple powers. Materials like feathers, beads, shells, or metals in each headdress might have been chosen not just for beauty, but for their spiritual or cosmological significance.

When worn together in processions or ceremonies, the four headdresses would have created a powerful visual narrative—a moving tableau of cultural identity, cosmic order, and communal values. Interpretation

Recovered from a domestic hut floor deposit with a small stone pigment grinder and a lump of carbon-based pigment (burial/habitat context; found with), the artifact situates tattooing within both ritual and daily life. The association with pigment suggests preparation, application, and ceremonial significance. This artifact comprises bird bone needles sharpened to fine points, bound in small clusters using plant fiber and resin adhesive (material/manufacture clues). Needle tips were polished to reduce skin trauma, and slight bending of some points and pigment residue indicate repeated use for tattooing (evidence of use). The binding method reflects care in organization and ritualized handling. The bundle measures 15 cm in total length, with individual needles between 7 and 12 cm, weighing 38 g (dimensions/physical properties). Minor fiber fraying is present, but the cluster structure remains intact (preservation state), preserving the relationship between needles, adhesive, and binding materials.
